Lesson 05 — Installing the Controller and Power Supply

Source: DeltaV M-Series Hardware Installation and Reference Manual, Emerson D800001X312, April 2021, pp. 39–44.

With I/O cards and terminal blocks installed (Lesson 04), it is time to mount the brain and the heart of the system — the DeltaV controller and its dedicated power supply. These two components sit together on the 2-wide power/controller carrier and form the core of the control system. The controller executes your control logic, manages I/O scanning, and communicates with workstations over the Control Network. The power supply converts line power into the clean 12 VDC that drives both the controller and the I/O cards.

5.1 Installing the DeltaV Controller

The controller is the central processing unit of the DeltaV system. For simplex controller systems, the controller mounts on the right slot of the 2-wide power/controller carrier. The left slot is reserved for the system power supply — this arrangement is fixed and cannot be swapped.

  1. Align the connectors on the back of the controller with the connectors on the right slot of the 2-wide power/controller carrier.
  2. Push the controller to attach it firmly to the carrier.
  3. Tighten the mounting screw to secure the controller in place.
Figure 2-13: Controller installation — the controller mounts on the right slot of the 2-wide power/controller carrier. Align the back connectors and push firmly to seat. (D800001X312, p. 40)
Key Concept — Fixed Slot Assignment: In a 2-wide power/controller carrier, the power supply always goes on the left and the controller always goes on the right. This is a hardware constraint — swapping them will not work and can damage the connectors. For a 4-wide power/controller carrier (used with Vertical carriers), refer to Appendix M in the manual.
💡 Tip — UPS Backup: The controller should be backed up by an uninterruptible power supply (UPS) to maintain current process data during short-term power outages. The UPS allows the controller to continue operating even when AC main power is down. Refer to the DeltaV Power and Grounding manual for proper UPS configuration and to the UPS manufacturer's specifications for runtime and capacity requirements.

If you are building a redundant controller system, a second controller is installed alongside the first on the carrier. Refer to the "Controller Redundancy" section in the manual for the dual-controller configuration details.

Ref: D800001X312, Section 2.5.9, pp. 39–40

5.2 Installing the System Power Supply

The system power supply takes line power (AC or DC) and converts it to the 12 VDC required to drive the controller and I/O cards. It mounts on either slot of the 2-wide power/controller carrier. The system power supply comes in two variants: AC/DC (converts AC line power to 12 VDC) and Dual DC/DC (passthrough from a bulk 12 VDC supply).

5.2.1 AC/DC System Power Supply — Simplex Wiring

For a simplex AC/DC installation, connect the input supply wires to the input power connection on the top of the system power supply:

  • Line (L) — from AC power distribution terminal block
  • Neutral (N) — from AC power distribution terminal block
  • Protective Earth (G) — to the DC ground bus in the enclosure
Figure 2-14: Simplex wiring diagram for system power supply (AC/DC) — top view showing Line, Neutral, and Protective Earth connections from the AC power distribution terminal block. (D800001X312, p. 41)
⚠️ Critical Warning — Hot-Plugging Power: Always remove input power to the supply before connecting or disconnecting the input power connection. The connector should not interrupt current flow and could be damaged if actuated under a load condition. This applies to both initial installation and any subsequent maintenance.

5.2.2 AC/DC System Power Supply — Redundant Wiring

For redundant AC/DC power supplies, each supply connects to separate terminal blocks. For optimum power redundancy, connect each system power supply to separate power disconnect panels, powered by separate isolation transformers. This ensures that a single power source failure does not bring down both supplies.

Figure 2-15: Redundant wiring diagram for system power supply (AC/DC) — use separate terminal blocks for each supply. Only one ground wire is needed because the return sides are internally connected. (D800001X312, p. 42)
Key Concept — Single Ground for Redundant Supplies: Only one ground wire is necessary for redundant AC/DC power supplies because the return side of both DC power supplies is internally connected to the ground terminal of either terminal strip. Use the left-hand terminal screw only. Connecting both supplies to protective earth can create a ground loop.
💡 Tip: If you are using a Two-Wide Power Carrier and redundant AC-to-DC power supplies, connect only one of the power supplies to protective earth. Connecting both can create a ground loop that introduces noise into the system and may cause erratic behavior.

5.2.3 Dual DC/DC System Power Supply

The dual DC/DC system power supply takes 12 VDC input (from a bulk power supply) and provides pass-through power to the carrier. This is used in systems where a central bulk DC supply distributes power to multiple carriers.

Figure 2-16: Simplex wiring diagram for the dual DC/DC system power supply — the fuse block is optional but recommended when multiple DC/DC supplies share the same bulk power supply. (D800001X312, p. 43)

⚠️ Pitfall — Dual DC/DC Grounding: Do not connect the ground terminal on the power carrier when powering the dual DC/DC system power supply from a 12 VDC supply. Doing so can create ground loops and interfere with proper system operation. Always use pre-formed metal shorting bars to ensure a good connection — never use individual jumper wires.
Ref: D800001X312, Section 2.5.10, pp. 40–43

5.3 Installing Alarm Contact Wiring

The system power supply includes alarm contacts that can be wired to a digital input card for power monitoring. This provides an early warning mechanism: if the power supply fails, the alarm relay opens, which can be detected by a DI card before the system actually goes down.

Figure 2-17: System power supply alarm contact wiring — the alarm relay contact is shown open (unpowered/shelf condition) but is closed during normal operation. Multiple supplies can be daisy-chained to a single DI card. (D800001X312, p. 43)
Key Concept — Alarm Contact Logic: The alarm relay is closed during normal operation. If the power supply fails, the relay opens, which can be detected by a digital input card connected to a 24 VDC external power supply. This provides an early warning of power supply failure before the system actually loses power. Multiple system power supplies can be daisy-chained to a single DI card for centralized monitoring.
Ref: D800001X312, Section 2.5.10, p. 43

5.4 Mounting the Power Supply in the Carrier

After wiring the input power and alarm contacts, the final step is to physically mount the power supply:

  1. Align the system power supply with the connector on the 2-wide power/controller carrier.
  2. Push to attach the supply firmly to the carrier.
  3. Tighten the mounting screw to secure it.
Figure 2-18: System power supply installation — align with the carrier connector and secure with the mounting screw. (D800001X312, p. 44)
💡 Tip — Ready for Transportation: Once the controller and power supply are installed and the hardware is mounted in an enclosure as described in the manual, the DeltaV hardware is ready for transportation. Additional preparation measures are not recommended and may actually damage the hardware. If you need to ship the system, follow the packaging guidelines in the manual.
Ref: D800001X312, Section 2.5.11, p. 44

Quiz — Lesson 05

Knowledge Check

Q1: In a 2-wide power/controller carrier, which slot holds the controller and which holds the power supply?

The power supply always goes on the left slot and the controller always goes on the right slot. This assignment is fixed by the hardware design and cannot be swapped.

Q2: Why should you always remove input power before connecting or disconnecting the power supply input connector?

The connector should not interrupt current flow and could be damaged if actuated under a load condition. Removing power first prevents arcing and connector damage that could render the power supply unsafe or non-functional.

Q3: How many ground wires are needed for redundant AC/DC power supplies, and why?

Only one ground wire is necessary because the return side of both DC power supplies is internally connected to the ground terminal of either terminal strip. Use the left-hand terminal screw only. Connecting both to protective earth can create a ground loop.

Q4: What is the state of the alarm relay contact during normal operation?

The alarm relay contact is closed during normal operation. It opens when the power supply fails, which can be detected by a digital input card. This provides an early warning before the system actually loses power.

Q5: What precaution must be taken when powering a dual DC/DC system power supply from a 12 VDC source?

Do not connect the ground terminal on the power carrier when powering from a 12 VDC supply. Doing so can create ground loops and interfere with proper system operation. Always use pre-formed metal shorting bars instead of individual jumper wires.

Q6: What are the two variants of the DeltaV system power supply, and what are their input types?

The two variants are: (1) AC/DC power supply, which converts AC line power to 12 VDC, and (2) Dual DC/DC power supply, which provides 12 VDC passthrough from a bulk power supply. The AC/DC variant is for standalone installations; the DC/DC variant is for multi-carrier systems with a central bulk supply.

Q7: Why is a UPS recommended for the controller?

A UPS allows the controller to continue operating and maintain current process data during short-term power outages. Without UPS backup, a power outage could cause the controller to lose its running state, potentially disrupting process control. Refer to the DeltaV Power and Grounding manual for proper UPS configuration.
